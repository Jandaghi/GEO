Gradient Erosion Optimization (GEO): A Geomorphology-Inspired Metaheuristic for Continuous and Combinatorial Optimization

Abstract
As real-world optimization problems grow in complexity, exact solution methods frequently become impractical, motivating the use of metaheuristic approaches. Although numerous metaheuristics have been proposed each characterized by a distinct metaphor and set of operators this paper introduces Gradient Erosion Optimization (GEO), a method that adopts a fundamentally different perspective. GEO is conceptually grounded in geomorphological processes of the Earth's crust, specifically the coupled effects of geological erosion, sedimentation, and tectonic faulting. The paper details the mechanisms responsible for exploration (diversification) and exploitation (intensification), together with their adaptive balance, governed by a time-varying decay function. The efficacy of GEO is evaluated on a comprehensive suite of mathematical and engineering benchmark problems widely adopted in the metaheuristic community. Its performance is systematically compared with standard implementations of several well-established algorithms; results indicate consistent competitive advantages across the tested instances. Convergence behavior and statistical validity are further examined through nonparametric significance tests, supporting the robustness of GEO. The experimental findings suggest that GEO constitutes a promising metaheuristic, well suited to a broad spectrum of continuous and combinatorial optimization challenges.
Keywords: Gradient Erosion Optimization; Combinatorial optimization; Metaheuristic algorithm; Continuous optimization; Nature-inspired algorithm.
